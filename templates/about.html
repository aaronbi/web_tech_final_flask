About Climb Time

Welcome to Climb Time! This is a website for planning/organizing outdoor climbing trips. On the home page you can find a list of planned outdoor climbing trips. In each trip page you can find information about the trip such as the destination of the trip, climbers that are going along with their skills and gear, planned routes for the trip, information about those routes, and some photos of the location.

Basic Information About Climbing

There are 3 main types of climbing outdoors: Bouldering, sport climbing, and trad climbing. Bouldering usually involves shorter routes where the climber doesn't need to tie into a rope and instead just uses a mat or pad below the route to soften the fall. Sport climbing routes are longer and require the climber to tie into a rope which is managed by a belayer. In sport climbing routes the rock already has bolts that have been fixed at various points along the route, so the climber only needs to bring quickdraws to clip into each bolt. These bolts are usually drilling and cemented into the wall, so generally they are very stable and strong. This contrasts with trad climbing which requires the climber to take specialized gear up the wall and place pieces of gear into various points (usually cracks in he wall) along the route as they ascend. This form of climbing is inherently more complex and dangerous for the climber, since they are responsible for how well they place their gear and certain parts of the route may not have any good places to put gear.

There are also some singificant differences between climbing inside at a gym and outside on real rock. Generally climbing outside is more dangerous since it is less of a controlled environment. When bouldering outside, climbers will have to bring their own pads to the boulder and will often need a spotter to make sure the climber lands on a pad if they fall. All longer routes outside will either be sport climbing routes or trad routes. Both trad and sport climbing require someone to lead climb the route first. Sport and trad routes will also require the climbers to bring their own gear (quickdraws for sport routes and trad gear for trad routes) and clean the routes afterwards to retrieve the gear that they placed on the way up.

The grade of a climb will indicate its difficulty in comparison to other climbs. There are a couple common grading scales used in climbing. French grades are used for both sport climbing and bouldering. YDS (Yosemite Decimal System) grades are used for trad and sport routes (mostly in America). V grades are used for bouldering. All of these grades use numbers and the higher the number the harder the climb. Generally grades outdoors will be harder than grades indoors. So the same boulder graded V5 indoors might be graded V3 outdoors. Difficulty of grades also tend to vary from gym to gym and area to area.

Climbing Terminology

Boulder or Boulder Problem
Shorter routes intended to be climbed without a rope. Pads are typically used to cushion any falls
Lead Climbing
Climbing the route first while placing gear or clipping into bolts for protection
Top Rope Climbing
Climbing a route where the rope is already fixed at the top of the route
Sport Route
A route that has bolts fixed into the wall that climbers can clip into for protection
Trad (Traditional) Route
A route with no bolts. Climbers will have to place their own protection into the wall as they climb
Belayer
The person that manages the rope and catches the climber during a fall on roped climbs (trad, sport, lead, top rope)
